Winter 2023 Work Term Report

  Dear reader, in this article I will discuss my time at Desire2Learn (D2L) over the 2023 summer semester. I worked as a full stack developer and really enjoyed my time with the team. My work was interesting, the culture was excellent and I would go back in a heartbeat. As you read on I hope you’ll see why I enjoyed my time so much.

<About-Desire2Learn/>

  D2L is the second largest company I’ve worked at, only second to Phenom which has a slightly larger headcount. D2L has been around for a while, more than 20 years in fact, and as of when I’m writing this D2L employs nearly 1200 employees and provides services to over 16 million learners. D2L is most known for its online learning platform, Brightspace which is used by small classrooms, universities, and everything in between across the world. As a matter of fact, Guelph has its own instance of Brightspace for distributing its course organization online , instead of Brightspace we call it Courselink.

  Now Brightspace is pretty neat and all but what’s really cool is D2L's latest product, Wave. Wave is an online professional upskilling platform built to streamline the process of connecting individuals with universities, colleges, and certification programs so that they can upgrade their professional self and progress their careers. Wave only recently became available to businesses in the last three years, whereas Brightspace has been around basically since D2L started over 20 years ago. It’s also worth mentioning that Wave is only run by one team of ~10 developers, which is where I found myself this term. In comparison, Brightspace consists of many teams each with a significant number of developers. D2L also recently opened a large office in the Catalyst building in downtown Waterloo, it’s pretty spectacular and I really enjoyed the few times I was able to go in person.

<Goals/>

  Now that you’ve got the scoop on D2L, Brightspace, and Wave let’s talk about a couple goals I had for the term. Now that it’s my fifth co-op term I feel like I’ve gotten a chance to experiment with lots of different goals over my four previous terms. This term I opted to go with more of what I would call ‘relaxed’ goals. I get that part of having goals is that they should be specific, actionable and measurable which these goals still are. The difference is that in the past I’ve put my goals pretty far out ahead of me, and occasionally I’ve stressed myself out trying to achieve them. Therefore this term I wanted to still set good goals which would help me improve, but I wanted them to be pretty attainable and reasonable. So let’s talk about the goals I set.

  • Number one was that I wanted to focus a lot on being a team player. This was a bit of an odd one because part of my goal was specifically to try and help people on my team learn new things or to help them solve problems and squish bugs. But I’m just an intern, what do I know that they don’t? Just kidding, kind of. At this point I feel like I know a fair bit about web development and so I really wanted to put my knowledge to work and help lift others around me up.
  • The next goal was very simple. I wanted to get more exposure to AWS stuff. Going into the term I knew that Wave was based pretty heavily in AWS infrastructure so if I could get exposure to multiple AWS services and really start to get an understanding of how everything fits together I felt like that would be very beneficial for me.
  • The final goal I had in mind was to get more experience reviewing PRs, particularly more complicated or large PRs. This has been a goal I’ve had across multiple terms and I put a lot of emphasis on it because I truly believe it's a pretty different set of skills required to be really good at reviewing someone else’s work. To build these skills up requires practice, hence me making this one of my goals.

  Well there you go, those are my goals for the term. Pretty simple and attainable wouldn’t you say? We’ll see. In the following two sections I’ll detail some of the cool stuff I got to do and then we’ll reflect on my goals and see how attainable they really were.

<My-Job/>

  This is the fun stuff, here you’ll get to see what I actually did for the last four months. First things first, onboarding. It’s a critical piece of the new employee experience and honestly I had a lot of fun with this part. There’s the usual mandatory courses about security and company culture but then I got right into work. One thing I appreciate about my team at D2L and luckily the same goes for all my previous teams at other companies is that they didn’t treat me like an intern. They treated me like any other dev, which is exactly what I enjoy. Also, during the first week I got paired up with an ‘onboarding buddy’, this person volunteers to get annoyed by the new employee during their first couple weeks on the job. Whether it be technical questions or asking how the coffee machine in the office works, your onboarding buddy has it covered. In all seriousness having someone who you can shamelessly ask many questions to is super beneficial and really speeds up the learning and ramp-up process in my opinion.

  From here on out I’ll refer to the team I worked with as team ‘Nova’, since that’s the team’s actual name. Nova works in six week ‘sprints’ or ‘product cycles’. The team splits into two smaller teams during this time and each team completes a major change or feature over the course of six weeks. After those six weeks we have two weeks of cooldown. This two week span is dubbed ‘dragonslayer’, still not totally sure where the name came from. The point of these two weeks is to try and clean up tech debt and work on any cool improvements that team members thought up during the product cycles.

  My first product cycle started a week after I joined. Which was nice because I was able to do my onboarding and then start on a fresh cycle the following week. My team of five devs decided to call ourselves team ‘unicorn-confetti’. Not sure where the name came from but we really tried to embody the spirit of unicorn-confetti. Our task was to implement a new widget that would be present in many parts of Wave. This widget would allow users to easily recommend and share courses that they planned to take or had already completed with their peers. Design work was already done so we got off to work right away. My first week was spent doing a lot of reading and watching. Any opportunity to join a huddle and watch someone explain some code, I was there.

  After this week of reading I put up my first PR for a small bug fix. It got accepted and then I hopped into more unicorn-confetti related work. I had to add some logic to fire Amplitude (analytics) events when users interacted with the widget in various ways. This was a fun task and I was able to poke around a bit in the frontend and backend of the app. Following that we got to work on animations. Wave didn’t have animations anywhere else at the time so this was a fun thing to work on and required lots of experimenting. The animations that we were using are Lottie animations, and implementing them in Lit-Elements was a bit of a struggle but we came up with a really clean and accessible solution that could be reused in other parts of the app quite easily. We continued developing and eventually got the feature merged right before the product cycle was up.

  During my first dragonslayer I checked out our team's documents of stuff that needed to be done. One of the things listed under ‘engineering goals’ was to create some strategy around frontend state management. That sounded pretty up my ally so to speak. So I spent a couple afternoons building out some documentation about what I thought of our frontend state management at the moment and what I thought could be done to improve it. State is a big deal in React but since the team uses Lit, everything looked a little bit different despite many of the principles being the same.

  I stirred up some discussion over the frontend and during the two week cooldown I was able to create a plan for how we would improve the frontend, I really enjoyed this and felt like I was doing something significant in regards to making positive change for the team.

  I’ll try and speed this up a bit since I’ve already typed out so many words but here I was, ready to start the next product cycle. This time I got placed on team ‘fire’ who was responsible for removing a bunch of old code in order to clean up the logic for features which would be coming in future product cycles. This time the team name made sense, fire, cleaning up code, setting it on fire. Okay, maybe it doesn’t make complete sense because removing code is much more technical than just setting it on fire. I think it makes more sense than unicorn-confetti but what do I know? Anyhow, I did just that. I spent most of the cycle removing code. It was an interesting experience and gave me a unique perspective on how much thought is required to remove a small bit of logic within a large system without screwing something somewhere else up. Towards the end of this cycle we wrapped up the code removal a tad bit early so I spent some more time working on the frontend state management docs.

  During the final week of the cycle I got to become the ‘supernova’. For comedic purposes I was tempted to leave that sentence without an explanation but I guess you might actually be curious what the heck a ‘supernova’ is in this context. Well, Wave does weekly deployments to prod. Usually on Tuesdays. One person every week is the designated supernova, this person is essentially an on call dev whose only focus is to ensure Wave is up and running and that the deployment goes smoothly, this person is also in charge of resolving any day to day support tickets and issues that are beyond the capabilities of our admins/support members. Since this week tends to be pretty hectic, the supernova becomes the ‘neutron star’ in the week following their supernova shift. As a neutron star, your days are much slower paced and you tend to work on a specific backlog of items that need to be cleaned up and that are suitable for one person to do over the course of a week.

  So yeah, that’s basically how those two weeks went. I got to release Wave V50.5.0 and proceeded to do a handful of hotfixes during the week. This was the largest quantity of PRs that the team had ever had in a release so there were a few rocky days during the week but all in all it went pretty smoothly. I don’t think many interns anywhere get to run a weekly production deployment. It’s certainly a unique experience that I was very fortunate to have. Make no mistake, it took a lot of hard work over the term to prove to my manager that I was capable of such responsibility. I'm glad that the work paid off and that I was able to have this experience.

  That was more or less the gist of what I did at D2L. I skipped on quite a few details for the sake of brevity but trust that I learned and did a lot at D2L and that my time here was truly an incredible opportunity.

<Reflections/>

  Now that you’re up to speed with what exactly I did over the last four months lets go look at those goals I talked about many paragraphs ago.

  My first goal related to be a better team player. This was honestly really easy to achieve and I’d like to attribute it primarily to my team’s culture. Everyone on the team was incredibly easy going and open to help. This made it easy for me to find the help I needed when I needed it but it also gave me the chance to easily help others when they needed it. Overall, I was really happy with this goal as I sort of achieved it naturally due to the way my team was set up. The next goal was to get more exposure to AWS tech. I think I crushed this goal. I got to play with all sorts of stuff. Messing around in Dynamo, exploring traffic and logs in our Lambdas, replaying events in our SQS dead letter queue. There’s so many areas of AWS that I didn’t even know existed that the team uses.

  The most exposure I got was during my time as supernova because I was constantly on the lookout for alarms and trying to understand why and how something was breaking. This typically required me to navigate multiple AWS services to resolve a problem, and once I got the hang of it I felt great. AWS was a very daunting thing in the past and now that I have some knowledge of it I’m excited to see what my future work with AWS will entail. Finally my last goal was to review large and more complicated PRs. This goal was actually really hard to achieve, and I would say I missed on this one. The reason it was hard to achieve is that the team does such a good job of breaking down tasks and tickets that no PR was ever so significant that it seemed intimidating. Everyone usually left steps on how to test their PR as well as a short explanation of their changes. The PRs that I had to review this term were some of the easiest to understand and review that I’ve ever seen primarily because they were so well divided and documented. All in, I’m pretty happy with how my goals turned out despite missing the mark on the last one. In hindsight perhaps I should have modified the goal to better fit my environment, that’s something for me to consider in the future.

<Summary/>

  In conclusion, I had an amazing time at D2L working on team Nova. Wave is an incredible product and I can’t wait to see it succeed in the coming years. My team was fantastic and I can’t get over how friendly and welcoming everyone was. I truly felt right at home with this team. Over the last four months I learned a lot, laughed a lot, and hope that someday I’ll find my way back to the offices of D2L.
